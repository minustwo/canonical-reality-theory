# 3. Types of Non-Determinism

## 3.1 Overview

In the present framework, non-determinism is tied to the admissible interpretation family associated with a structure theory \(T\). Writing \(\mathcal{P}_T\) for that family, the simplest indicator of non-determinism is plurality:
\[
|\mathcal{P}_T| > 1.
\]
When this condition holds, the theory admits more than one admissible interpretation. This does not imply inconsistency. It indicates only that admissibility, as currently specified, does not reduce the system to a unique outcome.

We distinguish between different sources of such plurality. The purpose of this section is classificatory rather than eliminative. We do not claim that every instance of non-determinism falls neatly into a single type, nor that the categories introduced below are exhaustive in all settings. The aim is instead to separate structurally different causes of plurality so that later determinization analysis can remain sensitive to source rather than treating all multiplicity as uniform.

## 3.2 Structural plurality

We use the term structural plurality for plurality that arises from the constraint system, admissibility rule, or representational structure of the theory itself. Informally, a theory exhibits structural plurality when multiple interpretations remain admissible even under fixed informational conditions.

In this case, non-determinism is not primarily a consequence of missing inputs or temporary uncertainty. Rather, it is generated by the internal structure of the theory: the constraints permit multiple admissible interpretations, the admissibility rule does not distinguish among them, or the structural description is underdetermined in a way that is intrinsic to the system under consideration.

Structural plurality may therefore persist even when the relevant informational state is held fixed. If the same constraint structure and admissibility policy continue to admit more than one interpretation, then the source of non-determinism is structural in the sense relevant here.

This notion should be read at the level of framework analysis. We do not assume that structural plurality is permanent in every possible extension of the theory. We claim only that, relative to the theory as specified, multiplicity is generated by the structure itself rather than by informational incompleteness alone.

## 3.3 Epistemic plurality

We use the term epistemic plurality for plurality that arises from incomplete, partial, or evolving information. In this case, multiple interpretations are admissible not because the structural constraints themselves require multiplicity, but because the information available to the system does not yet suffice to reduce the admissible family further.

Epistemic plurality may therefore collapse under refinement. Additional observations, added premises, clarified inputs, or strengthened informational access may reduce the set \(\mathcal{P}_T\), potentially without altering the underlying admissibility framework in any fundamental way. What changes is not necessarily the structural form of the theory, but the informational state relative to which admissibility is being evaluated.

This does not mean that epistemic plurality is merely superficial. In many settings, incomplete information is a stable feature of the environment rather than a temporary inconvenience. The point is narrower: plurality of this kind is tied to informational limitation or evolution, and may be reduced by refinement even when the underlying structural scheme remains unchanged.

## 3.4 Separation of sources

The distinction between structural plurality and epistemic plurality matters because the two sources need not behave in the same way. If plurality is structural, then adding more information may leave the admissible family plural. If plurality is epistemic, then refinement may reduce multiplicity without changing the deeper structure of the theory.

We therefore distinguish source from manifestation. The manifestation is the same in the simplest case: \(|\mathcal{P}_T| > 1\). The source, however, may differ. Two theories may display plural admissibility for different reasons, and a single theory may contain both structural and epistemic elements simultaneously.

For this reason, the distinction introduced here should not be treated as universally exhaustive or mutually exclusive in all systems. Hybrid cases may occur. A theory may be structurally permissive while also being informationally incomplete, so that plurality reflects more than one contributing factor. The present classification is intended to mark analytically different directions of explanation, not to impose a rigid partition on every case.

The separation also helps prevent category mistakes. Without it, one may attempt to resolve structural plurality through informational refinement alone, or treat epistemic plurality as if it established an irreducibly plural theory. In both cases, the determinization analysis risks misidentifying the relevant source of multiplicity.

## 3.5 Implications for determinization

The distinction between sources of non-determinism has direct consequences for determinization analysis. If plurality is structural, then any route to determinization may require modification or strengthening of the admissibility framework itself, additional structural constraints, or a principled selection mechanism that is not already contained in the original theory. If plurality is epistemic, determinization may instead depend on refinement of information, clarification of inputs, or extension of what is available to the system at the point of evaluation.

Accordingly, determinization mechanisms may differ across types. A mechanism that is suitable for epistemic plurality need not address structural plurality, and a structural resolution strategy need not be the relevant response to informational incompleteness. The analysis of determinization should therefore begin with source classification rather than assuming that all plural admissible families are reducible in the same way.

This section does not provide a resolution procedure. Its role is to classify forms of non-determinism at a structural level and to indicate why that classification matters. The determinization problem is better posed when the source of plurality is made explicit, even if no immediate reduction mechanism is yet available.
