Core observation

The early CRT research workflow was not merely a fast writing process.

It was a structured research-control system.

The process combined:

* founding principles
* explicit repository governance
* status labels
* commit-level review
* adversarial audit
* open-question tracking
* public error correction
* AI-assisted execution
* human final authority

The working metaphor is:

theory operating system

This metaphor means that research artifacts were versioned, audited, labeled, and released under explicit rules.

It does not mean the repository is software.

Controlled phase map

The early development process can be described as a sequence of phases.

This phase map is a public-safe abstraction.

It does not reproduce private repository internals.

Phase 1 — Foundational principles

The process began by fixing guiding principles before expanding theory.

Purpose:

* constrain later claims
* discipline assumptions
* prevent uncontrolled theory growth
* give AI agents explicit boundaries

Public lesson:

A research program should define its constitution before expanding theorem space.

Phase 2 — Repository governance

A tiered repository system was used to separate:

* canonical material
* appendices
* status records
* review records
* conceptual notes
* supporting files
* deprecated artifacts

Public lesson:

File structure is part of research governance.

Phase 3 — Commit-level review

Review was applied at small increments.

A typical internal loop was:

draft
audit
patch
status update
commit
review

Public lesson:

Reviewer-style objections can be internalized before public release.

This does not replace external peer review.

Phase 4 — Pre-audit patches

Before some artifacts were written or released, likely objections were anticipated and patched.

Public lesson:

A strong artifact anticipates objections before release.

Phase 5 — Reviewer attack inventory

Potential reviewer objections were mapped before external exposure.

Public lesson:

A theorem or framework is not stable merely because it is coherent.
It must survive plausible attacks within its stated scope.

Phase 6 — Adversarial probes

The workflow emphasized attempts to break claims rather than only defend them.

Adversarial probing can produce:

* counterexamples
* boundary classes
* demotions
* stronger assumptions
* open problems

Public lesson:

A counterexample is a research artifact.

Phase 7 — Open-question ladders

Open questions were decomposed into smaller research rungs.

This allowed progress without pretending full closure.

Public lesson:

Partially resolved is a valid research state.

Phase 8 — Freeze decisions

When conflicting versions or unresolved dependencies appeared, the preferred action was to freeze the ambiguity rather than force a premature decision.

Public lesson:

When correctness cannot be judged, preserve the conflict rather than hide it.

Phase 9 — Public correction

Errors were treated as part of the research record.

Public lesson:

Transparent correction is stronger than hidden revision.

Phase 10 — Cross-domain bridge discipline

The workflow explored structural connections across different theory domains.

However, cross-domain alignment does not automatically establish equivalence.

Public lesson:

Unification claims must remain conditional unless separately proved.

Phase 11 — AI role separation

AI systems were used as:

* drafting assistants
* adversarial auditors
* repository execution agents
* cross-checking tools
* status-synthesis aids

AI systems were not treated as:

* final authorities
* peer reviewers
* proof certifiers
* institutional endorsers

Public lesson:

AI can assist the process.
Human maintainers remain responsible for acceptance.

Phase 12 — Sustainable pacing

High-intensity research periods can be useful but should not become permanent operating mode.

Public lesson:

Sustainable judgment is part of research quality.

Methodological invariant

The common invariant across the workflow is honesty.

In this repository, honesty means:

* preserving audit trails
* keeping status labels explicit
* admitting uncertainty
* recording errors
* demoting claims when needed
* preserving superseded artifacts
* separating proof from sketch
* separating audit from peer review
* separating public artifact from final theorem

The repository prefers:

conditional and honest

over:

stronger but unsupported

Reusable checklist

Before starting a new research line, ask:

1. What are the founding principles?
2. What status label applies?
3. What assumptions are explicit?
4. What would a hostile reviewer attack?
5. What pre-audit patches are needed?
6. What open questions remain?
7. What artifacts are superseded?
8. What errors must be preserved?
9. What AI roles were used?
10. What does the human maintainer accept?
11. Does this require context-reset audit?
12. Does this change theorem status?
